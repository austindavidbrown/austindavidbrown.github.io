Suppose $X,Y$ are metric spaces, $E \subset X$, $f:X \to Y$, and $p$ is a limit point of $E$. Then $$ \lim_{x \to p}{f(x)} = q \label{1}\tag{1} $$ iff for every sequence $(p_n) \subset E$ such that $p_n \neq p$ and $\lim{(p_n)} = p$, $$ \lim{f(p_n)} = q \label{2}\tag{2} $$ Proof. Suppose (1). Then let $(p_n) \subset E$ satisfy $p_n \neq p$, and $\lim{(p_n)} = p$. Let $\epsilon > 0$. By (1), set $\delta > 0$ such that $\forall x : d(x, p) < \delta \implies d(f(x), q) < \epsilon$. Since $(p_n) \to p$, set $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $\forall n : N \leq n \implies d(p_n, p) < \delta$. Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$, and suppose $N \leq n$. Then set $p_n = x$, and hence $N \leq n \implies d(p_n, p) < \delta \implies d(f(p_n), q) < \epsilon$. Conversely, we prove the contrapositive. Suppose (2). Then suppose (1) is false by contradiction. Then set $\epsilon_1$ such that $\forall \delta > 0$ $\exists x$ such that $d(x, p) < \delta$ and $d(f(x), q) \ge \epsilon_1$. Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$. Then set $\delta_n = 1/n$. Then set $x_n$ such that $d(x_n, p) < \delta_n$ and $d(f(x_n), q) \ge \epsilon_1$. Now, since $f(x_n) \not\to q$, we just need to prove $(x_n) \to p$ and this will contradict (2) and prove the contrapositive. Let $\epsilon > 0$. Then set $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $1/N < \epsilon$. Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$, and suppose $N \leq n$. Then $1/n \leq 1/N$ and $d(x_n, p) < 1/n$ $\leq 1/N < \epsilon$. Therefore, $(x_n) \to p$ and $f(x_n) \not\to q$, a contradiction to (2) as desired. $\Box$ Remark. This theorem was difficult for me. The main idea here is possibly lost I think.
