Definition. $f : A \to B$ is epic means for all $Z, g, g'$ such that $g, g' : B \to Z$, $g \circ f = g' \circ f \implies g = g'$. Thm. $f$ is epic iff $f$ is surjective. Proof. Suppose $f$ is epic. Then suppose $f$ is not surjective by contradiction. Then there exists some $b_0$ such that $\forall a, b_0 \not= f(a)$. Set $h : B \cup \{b_1\} \to B$ such that $h(b) = b$ and $h' : B \cup \{b_1\} \to B$ such that $h'(b) = \begin{cases} b & b \not= b_0\\\ b_1 & b = b_0 \end{cases}$. Then by construction of these two functions and the hypothesis $f(a) \not= b_0$ ever, $h \circ f = h' \circ f$. Then this implies $h = h'$ since $f$ is epic, a contradiction, so $f$ is surjective. Suppose $f$ is surjective. Then suppose for all $Z, g, g'$ such that $g, g' : B \to Z$, and suppose $g \circ f = g' \circ f$. Since $f$ is surjective, there exists a right inverse, and hence $$ (g \circ f) \circ f^{-1} = (g' \circ f) \circ f^{-1} \\\ g = g'. $$ Therefore, $f$ is epic and has the right cancellation property.
