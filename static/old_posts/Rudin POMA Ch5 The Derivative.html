Definition. The Derivative. Let $f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$. Then $\forall x \in [a, b]$, $$ f'(x) = \lim_{t \to x}{ \frac{f(x) - f(t)}{x - t}} $$ is the derivative at x where it exists. There is really no point in copying Rudin's definition, but I put it here anyhow. Theorem. Differentiability implies Continuity. Let $f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be differentiable at $x \in [a, b]$. Then $f$ is continuous at $x$. I will try to fill in a couple blanks left in the proof. Proof. Since f is differentiable at x, it is a limit point, so we use Theorem 4.6. We need to prove $\lim_{t \to x} f(t)$ = f(x). Hence, by the properties of limits, $$ \lim_{t \to x} f(t) - f(x) = \lim_{t \to x} \frac{f(t) - f(x)}{x - t} (x - t) = f'(x) \lim_{t \to x} t - x = f'(x) \cdot 0 = 0. $$. Hence, using the properties of limits again, $$ \lim_{t \to x} f(t) - f(x) = 0 \implies \lim_{t \to x} f(t) = f(x) $$ This proof is very trivial, but kind of confusing to actually write out for some reason.
