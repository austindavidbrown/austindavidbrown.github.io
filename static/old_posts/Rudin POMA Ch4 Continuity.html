A little different version is $\forall B_{\epsilon}(f(p)), \exists B_{\delta}(p) : \forall x \in B_{\delta}(p), f(x) \in B_{\epsilon}(f(p))$ This just gives a much nicer geometric idea of what is going on. Theorem. Continous maps preserve composition. Proof. We have to cheat and use Theorem 4.8. Let $X, Y, Z$ be metric spaces, and $f, g$ be continuous maps such that $X \xrightarrow{f} Y \xrightarrow{g} Z$. Let $B$ be an open set in $Z$. Then $g^{-1}(B)$ is open since g is continuous, and $f^{-1}(g^{-1}(B))$ = $(g \circ f)^{-1}(B)$ is open since f is continuous. By Theorem 4.8, $(g \circ f)$ is continuous. $\Box$ This proof is super slick. I saw this in Francis Su's awesome lecture series. I think he has a very nice way of explaining things personally. Theorem. Topological Characterization to Contunuous Maps. Let $f$ map the metric space $X$ to the metric space $Y$. Then $f$ is continuous on $X$ iff $\forall$ open set $B$ in $Y$, $f^{-1}(B)$ is open in $X$. Proof. Let $f$ be continuous on $X$. Let $B \subset Y$ be open. We need to prove $f^{-1}(B)$ is open, so every point in $f^{-1}(B)$ is an interior point. Let $x \in f^{-1}(B)$. Then since B is open and $f(x) \in B$, $\exists \epsilon_1$ such that $B_{\epsilon_1}(f(x)) \subset B$. Since $f$ is continuous on $X$, and with $\epsilon_1$, $\exists \delta$ such that $\forall x' \in B_{\delta}(x), f(x') \in B_{\epsilon_1}(f(x)) \subset B \implies x' \in f^{-1}(B)$ as desired. Conversely, suppose $\forall$ open set $B$ in $Y$, $f^{-1}(B)$ is open in $X$. Then we need to prove $f$ is continuous. Let $p \in X$, and let $\epsilon > 0$. Then $f(p) \in Y$. Let $B_{\epsilon}(f(p))$ is open in $Y$. Then $f^{-1}(B_{\epsilon}(f(p)))$ is open by hypothesis, and $f(p) \in B_{\epsilon}(f(p)) \implies p \in f^{-1}(B_{\epsilon}(f(p)))$. Since $B_{\epsilon}(f(p))$ is open, and with $p$, $\exists \delta$ such that $B_{\delta}(p) \subset f^{-1}(B_{\epsilon}(f(p)))$. Therefore, $f(B_{\delta}(p)) \subset B_{\epsilon}(f(p))$, so $\forall x' \in B_{\delta}(p), f(x') \in B_{\epsilon}(f(p))$ as desired. Since $p$ was arbitrary, $f$ is continuous on $X$. $\Box$ This took me a bit to wrap my head around. It actually makes a lot of sense now.
