Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable and dense in $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{R}$ is a seperable topological space. Theorem. $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$. Proof. Let $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$. Then we use Trichotemy on $a \in \mathbb{R}$. Case: $0 \le a < b$. Then by the Archimedean Property, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $\frac{1}{n} < b - a$ implies $na + 1 < nb$. With this $n$, define S = $\{ n' \in \mathbb{N} : na < n' \}$. Hence, by Well Ordering Principle, $S$ is nonempty, and there exists $p \in S$ such that $p$ is smallest element of S. Hence, $p - 1 \not\in S$ and $p - 1 \le na < p$ implies $a < \frac{p}{n}$ and $p \le an + 1 < nb$ implies $\frac{p}{n} < b$. Combining the two inequalities, $a < \frac{p}{n} < b$ as desired. Case: $a < 0$. Then we use Trichotemy on $b \in \mathbb{R}$. Case: $0 < b$. Then by case 1, there exists $p, n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $ a < 0 < \frac{p}{n} < b$ as desired. Case: $b \le 0$. Then $0 < -b$, and $0 < -a$. Hence by case 1, there exists $p, n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $-b < \frac{p}{n} < -a$ implies $a < -\frac{p}{n} < b$ as desired. $\Box$ Well, that finishes this short post. On to the next!
