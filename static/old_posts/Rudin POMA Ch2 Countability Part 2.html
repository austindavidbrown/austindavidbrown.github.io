To start, there are two really useful theorems from Munkres [2] that make life very easy. I will write them out here without any proof since it is kind of complicated and Munkres does a great job. Theorem. Countability Criterian from Munkres [2]. The following are equivalent: (i) S is countable. (ii) There exists a surjective map $\varphi : \mathbb{N} \to B$ (iii) There exists a injective map $\varphi : B \to \mathbb{N}$ Proof. I really like Munkres proof [2]. Theorem. A finite cartesian product of countable sets is countable. Proof. Let $\Phi : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N}$ by $\Phi(m) = (m, n)$ is surjective by definition. Then let $A, B$ be countable sets, and hence there exists two bijective maps $\psi : \mathbb{N} \to A$ and $\omega : \mathbb{N} \to B$. Next, define $\Lambda : \mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{A} \times \mathbb{B}$ by $\Lambda(m, n) = (\psi(m), \omega(n))$ is surjective by definition. Hence, $\Lambda\circ \Phi : \mathbb{N} \to A \times B$ is surjective, and hence $A \times B$ is countable. Next, the remaining inductive argument for $n$ cartesian products is trivial and in Munkres [2]. Theorem. A countable union of countable sets is countable. Proof. The idea is to use the cartesian product. Also, I really like Munkres [2] proof! Theorem. $\mathbb{Z}$ is countable. Proof. Let $\mathbb{Z} = \{ 0 \} \cup \mathbb{N} \cup -\mathbb{N}$ is a countable union of countable sets. $\Box$ Theorem. $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable. Proof. Using Example 3 from Munkres [2], define $\varphi : \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q_+}$ by $\varphi(m, n) = m/n$ is surjective by definition. Then since $\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}$ is countable, there exists a surjective map $\psi : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}$. Hence $\varphi \circ \psi : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q_+}$ is a surjective map implies $\mathbb{Q_+}$ is contable . Next, $\mathbb{Q} = \{ 0 \} \cup \mathbb{Q_+} \cup -\mathbb{Q_+}$ is a countable union of countable sets. $\Box$ Theorem. $\mathbb{R}$ is uncountable. Proof. Skipping for now.
