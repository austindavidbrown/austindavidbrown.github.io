Exercise 2.10 For all $m \in \mathbb{Z}$, $m = qn + r$ for some unique $q, r$ such that $0 \le r < n$ by the Division Algorithm. Hence, $n \mid a - r$ implies $a \equiv r$ mod $n$, and $0 \le r < n$ is unique. Exercise 2.11 Really cool to realize the finiteness of modular arithmetic lends to proving by enumeration. Exercise 2.13 Suppose $\exists a, b$ such that $am + bn = 1$. Then $am \equiv 1 \mod n$, and so $m$ generates $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}, +)$ implies $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ by the order theorem. Conversely, suppose for all $a, b$, $am + bn \not= 1$. Then $am \not\equiv 1 \mod n$, and $m$ does not generate $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}, +)$ implies $gcd(m, n) \not=1$ by the order theorem.
