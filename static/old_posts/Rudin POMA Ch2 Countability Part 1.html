Let $U \subset \mathbb{N}$. If $U$ is finite, then we are done. Suppose $U$ is infinite. Then since we do not know $U$ is ordered, we enumerate $U$ with an index map. Since $U \subset \mathbb{N}$, let $U = \{u_i\}_{i \in I}$ such that $I \subset \mathbb{N}$. Then $u_1 \in U$, and since $U$ is infinite, for all $i \in I$, $u_i \in U$ implies $u_{i + 1} \in U$ implies $I = \mathbb{N}$ by induction. Hence, $U = \{u_i\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$. Next, we prove the index map is a bijection. For all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $n \not= n + 1$ implies $u_n \not= u_{n+1}$ since $U$ is infinite, and hence the index map is injective. Next, for all $u \in U = \{u_i\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$, $u = u_i$ for some $i \in \mathbb{N}$ implies the index map is surjective. Hence, the index map is bijective, and $U$ is countable. $\Box$ Theorem. All subsets of a countable set are countable. Proof. Let $A$ be a countable set. Then there exists a map $\varphi : A \to \mathbb{N}$ is bijective. Let $B \subset A$. If $B$ is finite, we are done. Suppose $B$ is infinite. Then let $\psi = \varphi \circ \iota_B : B \to \psi(B)$ such that $\iota_B$ is the inclusion map, and hence $\psi$ is bijective. Since $\psi(B) \subset \mathbb{N}$, $\psi(B)$ is countable, and there exists an $\omega : \psi(B) \to \mathbb{N}$ is bijective. Hence, $\omega \circ \psi : B \to \mathbb{N}$ is a composotion of bijections and is a bijection. $\Box$ Now this theorem implies that intersections will be countable since they are subsets.
